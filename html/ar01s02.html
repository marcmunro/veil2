2. What Is A Relational Security System?

A Relational Security System is one in which each assigned privilege has a scope, which limits the extent to which the privilege applies.

The scope of an assigned privilege is related to a specific subset of data, so that each privilege assignment will give access to that specific subset. The scope may be all-encompassing or very specific. It may give access to thousands of records; to just a few; or even to none. And privileges may be assigned multiple times, in different scopes.

Scopes may be created from almost any definable subset of your data. Mostly they are based on the relationships of your users with their data, though they need not be. Typically, scopes are defined in terms of parties and their relationships either to other parties, or to specific artifacts described by your database. For example, a person's relationship to the company they work for, or their membership of a project team.

Having scopes associated with privileges means that the granularity of access controls can be as coarse or as fine as the data demands, and it means that there is rarely the need for more complex access checks than "do I have privilege X in scope Y?".

Instead of having privileges which imply conditional rule checks, such as the privilege to maintain-a-project-if-the-user-is-a-member-of-the-project-team, privileges can effectively contain their conditionality through their scope.
